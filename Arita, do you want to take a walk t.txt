"Arita, do you want to take a walk to the forest tomorrow?"

I said the words while looking at the small space between our coffee cups. The morning was entirely ordinary, yet there was a specific weight to the light filling the room.

"I just got some free tickets from my colleagues. It is supposed to be a sunny and windy day tomorrow, and yeah, it's the weekend, too. It would be so nice to take a break and go for a walk," I told him.

I looked up to see his reaction. Arita paused his movements, a piece of toast held halfway to his mouth. He blinked, the faint fatigue of the school week still clinging to the corners of his eyes, but then a slow, very quiet smile spread across his face. He nodded. The simple agreement felt like a stone dropping into a calm pond, the ripples spreading out to touch the edges of the weekend ahead of us.

We are having breakfast today. It is a sunny day, too, but it has never felt this bright compared to the others. I know we are in the summer season now; the vivid feelings are starting to take hold. The sunlight hits the edge of our dining table, painting a harsh, bright rectangle against the wood. It catches the dust motes dancing in the air, suspending them like silver grains drifting through the lens of an old 16mm camera. Everything feels warm, slightly washed out, and incredibly present.

The TV is broadcasting the news, short clips of a morning film, and some conversations between the cast, but the sound is entirely drowned out because I am listening to Arita, sitting opposite of me. The voices from the screen rise and fall in a predictable rhythm, a low frequency that blends with the hum of the refrigerator and the distant noise of the traffic outside. I hear the soft clink of his spoon against porcelain, the rustle of his shirt as he leans forward, the steady sound of his breathing.

In the mornings, we do not really have much to do besides eating and heading out. We move around each other in the small space of the kitchen with a practiced ease. It has been a year, or maybe just around it, since we moved into this new apartment. A year of these mornings. A year of learning the exact shape of each other's routines.
When we first came here, there were boxes stacked in every corner, casting long shadows in the evenings. Many things have happened since then, mostly because of us realizing more about ourselves. We were two men trying to build a life in a space that did not have a blueprint. We were stepping outside the lines of what people expected, relying only on this quiet, persistent pull we felt toward each other. A pull that did not require a name to be real.

We leave the apartment together. The heat of the morning is already rising from the asphalt, creating a faint shimmer in the distance. We walk to the station, our shoulders occasionally brushing. We part ways at the platform, Arita heading toward his high school, and I toward my office. I watch his back disappear into the crowd.

The commute is a blur of passing trees and grey buildings. The vivid feelings of summer press against the windows of the train. I close my eyes and let the rocking motion lull me. The light flashes behind my eyelids, warm and red, like the light leaking through the edges of a film strip.

Then comes the workplace. The noons of emptying out.

The office is air-conditioned and still. Time moves differently here, stretching out until it feels entirely suspended. I look at the documents on my desk, the glow of the monitor reflecting in the glass beside me. The sounds of typing and muffled phone conversations fade into a drone, much like the sound of wind over an empty field. My mind empties out. It is a necessary contrast to the fullness of my life outside this building. I let the hours wash over me, untangling the knots of thought until there is only a flat, calm surface remaining.

As I sit in the quiet of the office, I think about the rhythm we have created. The mornings of rushing to the workplace, the noons of emptying out, the afternoons of returning home, and the nights of many conversations and difficulties.

We had to learn how to exist together. It was not always simple. There were nights when the air in the apartment felt too thick, when we struggled to communicate our boundaries, our fears of encroaching on one another. The difficulties were quiet, mostly. A misunderstanding of silence. A misreading of distance. We spent hours talking in the low light of the living room, trying to find the right words to describe a bond that lacked a traditional vocabulary. We had to break apart our own assumptions to understand what we actually needed from one another.

But overall, I love living with him. Maybe he gives me a way, a chance to actually feel like I can be myself. I do not have to put on a performance of a life I do not want. I do not have to force myself into shapes that do not fit. In the space we have built, I just have to be Takeda. And he is just Arita. That alone is enough.

The afternoon drags its feet, moving slow and heavy. I finish my tasks, watching the shadows lengthen across the floor of the office. When I finally leave the building, the sun is lower, casting a deep, golden wash over the city. The heat has baked into the concrete, radiating upward. The wind picks up, warm and dry, rustling the leaves of the trees lining the street. It sounds like static, a soft shushing sound that clears the mind of whatever debris the workday left behind.

I stop by a small store to buy some things for tomorrow. A couple of water bottles, some small bread rolls, a pack of tea. The mundane act of gathering supplies feels entirely different when you are doing it for two people, for a shared destination. I place the items in my bag, feeling a quiet anticipation settling in my chest.

I walk the rest of the way home. The afternoons of returning. I like the feeling of walking toward the apartment as the day begins to cool. I like knowing that the door will unlock to a space that belongs to us.

I arrive before him. The apartment is quiet, holding the trapped warmth of the day. I open the window in the living room. The summer wind blows the thin curtains inward, filling the room with the smell of evening air. I sit on the floor, leaning back against the sofa, listening to the distant hum of traffic and the occasional sound of a train passing miles away. The ambient noise wraps around me. It feels expansive, like standing in the middle of a vast, open plain where the wind plays the world like an instrument.

An hour later, I hear the sound of the lock turning.

Arita steps inside. He looks tired, his shoulders carrying the invisible weight of a long day of teaching, managing students, navigating the noise of the school. But when he takes off his shoes and looks up, his expression softens. He breathes out, a long exhalation that seems to release the tension from his frame.
"I am back," he says, his voice low.

"Welcome back," I reply.

We eat dinner together as the sky outside turns from bruised purple to a deep, absolute black. We talk about small things. A student who asked a strange question in his class. A heavy cloud I saw on my way back from the station. The conversation is easy, a steady stream that requires no effort to navigate. It is a relief to speak without the fear of being misunderstood.

After the dishes are washed and the table is wiped clean, we bring out my canvas bag. We sit on the rug in the living room.

We are packing our things for tomorrow.

I fold a small blanket and place it at the bottom of the bag, smoothing out the fabric. Arita puts the water bottles inside, his movements deliberate and careful. We check the weather forecast on his phone one more time. Sunny and windy. Perfect for the forest. I take the tickets from my pocket and slip them into the front compartment of the bag, ensuring the zipper is fully closed.

The physical act of packing is grounding. The rustle of nylon, the crinkle of plastic wrappers, the soft thud of items settling against each other. It is a ritual of preparation for a day of doing nothing but walking and looking. I watch his hands as he organizes the small items, his long fingers moving with a familiar grace. He looks up and catches me watching. He smiles, a small, genuine expression that anchors me entirely to this exact moment.

Night falls completely over the city. The crickets start their rhythmic chirping somewhere down in the small garden behind our building. The wind is still blowing outside, carrying the smell of dry earth and warm leaves. It reminds me of the grains of the field, somewhere far beyond the city limits, waiting for us to arrive.
I look at Arita, reading a book under the warm light of the floor lamp. The silence between us is a physical thing, but it is not heavy. It is supportive. It holds us up.

I feel like he will be the sail I can rest beside for hours, and I will be the person who keeps this place secretly for myself. Arita moves through the world with a forward momentum, catching the wind, pulling us toward new days and new experiences. I am the shore, constant and waiting, keeping our shared life safe from the changing tides. The shores are fulfilled by the waves washing over them, the sun shining endlessly across the expanse.

I realize I have found a place where the noise of the world cannot reach me, or at least, cannot harm me. The vivid feelings of summer are no longer overwhelming. They are just the backdrop to this quiet life.

And I am here with a gentle dream. A dream of walking through a forest with the person who understands the silence inside me.

We leave the packed bag by the door, ready for the morning. The apartment is completely dark now, save for the small lamp in the corner.

And I really hope it will be a great day.

Summer, please take my words gently. Let the wind tomorrow be kind. Let the sunlight filter through the trees in soft, golden patches. I know there is a place I want to go besides the forest, and the sail to the forest is already here.
